Topic 3: Basic Korean Markers

In this lesson, you will learn the basic Korean Markers.

What Are Korean Particles?

Korean particles, also called markers, are suffixes attached to nouns to indicate their grammatical function in a sentence. Unlike English which uses word order to show relationships, Korean uses these particles to mark subjects, objects, locations, and other grammatical roles. Mastering particles is fundamental to speaking and understanding Korean correctly.

Particle Usage Example Meaning
은/는 Topic marker

Marks the topic of the sentence, what you're talking about. Use 은 after consonants, 는 after vowels.
저는 학생이에요.
Jeoneun haksaengieyo.
As for me, I'm a student.
(Emphasizes "I" as the topic)
이/가 Subject marker

Marks the subject of the sentence, who performs the action. Use 이 after consonants, 가 after vowels.
고양이가 예뻐요.
Goyangiga yeppeyo.
The cat is pretty.
(Focuses on "cat" as the subject)
을/를 Object marker

Marks the direct object of the verb. Use 을 after consonants, 를 after vowels.
사과를 먹어요.
Sagwareul meogeoyo.
(I) eat an apple.
(Marks "apple" as what's being eaten)
에 Location/Time marker

Indicates direction (to/at a place) or time (at/in time).
학교에 가요.
Hakgyoe gayo.
(I) go to school.
(Shows direction toward school)
에서 Action location marker

Indicates where an action takes place (at/in a place).
집에서 공부해요.
Jibeseo gongbuhaeyo.
(I) study at home.
(Shows where studying happens)
와/과
하고
(이)랑 		"And/With" connector

Connects nouns meaning "and" or "with." 와/과 is formal, 하고 is neutral, (이)랑 is casual.
친구랑 놀아요.
Chingurang norayo.
(I) play with a friend.
(Connects "I" and "friend" together)

Choosing Between 은/는 vs 이/가

은/는 marks the topic (what you're talking about), while 이/가 marks the subject (who does the action). 은/는 often implies contrast or emphasis, while 이/가 is neutral.

에 vs 에서 Difference

Use 에 for direction (going to a place) or static location (being at a place). Use 에서 for actions happening at a location. Think: 에 = to/at, 에서 = at/in (while doing something).

Consonant/Vowel Rules

Many particles have two forms: use the consonant-ending version (은, 이, 을, 와) after consonants, and vowel-ending version (는, 가, 를, 과) after vowels. This makes pronunciation easier.
